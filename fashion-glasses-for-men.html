Key Components: Sphere, Cylinder, Axis, Add

Every prescription for glasses contains specific measurements that address your unique visual needs. These details define the power and orientation of your lens materials for glasses (polycarbonate, Trivex, nylon).

  • Sphere (SPH): This indicates the primary power of your prescription glasses prescription, measured in diopters. A minus sign (-) signifies nearsightedness (myopia), meaning you have difficulty seeing distant objects clearly. A plus sign (+) denotes farsightedness (hyperopia), where close-up vision is challenging.
  • Cylinder (CYL): This value, also in diopters, specifies the amount of lens power needed to correct astigmatism. Astigmatism is a common condition caused by an irregularly shaped cornea or lens, leading to blurred or distorted vision at all distances.
  • Axis: The axis number ranges from 1 to 180 degrees and indicates the orientation of the astigmatism correction. It defines the angle at which the cylindrical power is applied to your corrective eyewear lens.
  • Add (Addition): If you require multifocal or progressive prescription glasses prescription (often for presbyopia, a natural age-related loss of near focusing ability), this value indicates the added magnifying power for reading or close-up work. This "add" power is always a positive number.
  • Prism (less common): In some cases, a prism value might be included to correct eye alignment issues, such as strabismus. It helps to ensure that your eyes work together correctly, preventing double vision or eye strain with your vision aids.

The Importance of Pupillary Distance for Glasses

Beyond the optical power, a critical measurement for your prescription for glasses is your pupillary distance for glasses (PD). This is the exact measurement in millimeters between the centers of your pupils.

If the PD is incorrect, even slightly, it can lead to distorted vision, eye strain, headaches, and overall discomfort, regardless of how accurate your Sphere, Cylinder, and Axis values are. Polarized vs Non-Polarized Prescription Lenses: Understanding Glare Reduction and Depth Perception

The choice between polarized vs non-polarized prescription lenses primarily impacts how your prescription glasses prescription handle glare and perceived depth.

Polarized lenses are engineered with a special filter that blocks horizontal light waves, which are responsible for reflective glare from surfaces like water, snow, roads, and car hoods. Conversely, non-polarized prescription lenses do not filter out horizontal light waves. While they offer protection from brightness, they do not eliminate glare. Non-polarized lenses are often preferred when viewing LCD screens (e.g., car dashboards, phone screens, ATMs), as polarized lenses can cause these screens to appear blacked out. They also allow for better judgment of icy patches on roads or slopes, and for activities requiring precise depth perception.
