How to Read Your Eye Prescription: Key Terms Explained

Decoding your eye prescription might seem daunting initially, but it's straightforward once you understand the key terms. Knowing these terms is crucial for ensuring your cheap prescription glasses are crafted correctly.

  • +/- Signs: These symbols indicate the type of vision problem. A plus sign (+) signifies farsightedness (hyperopia), meaning you have difficulty seeing close-up objects. A minus sign (-) denotes nearsightedness (myopia), indicating trouble seeing distant objects clearly.
  • OS and OD: These are Latin abbreviations. O.S. stands for "oculus sinister," referring to your left eye. O.D. stands for "oculus dexter," referring to your right eye. Sometimes, "O.U." (oculus uterque) might be used for both eyes.
  • SPH (Sphere): This number indicates the main lens strength required to correct your vision. It's measured in diopters and will have a plus or minus sign.
  • CYL (Cylinder): This term refers to the cylindrical correction needed for astigmatism. If you have astigmatism, your eye's cornea or lens has an irregular curvature.
  • ADD (Addition): The "ADD" power is used for progressive or bifocal lenses and indicates the additional magnification needed for reading or close-up work. This is typically a positive number.
  • AXIS: If you have astigmatism (a CYL value), the AXIS indicates the orientation or location of the astigmatism on the lens. It's represented by a number between 1 and 180 degrees.

Understanding these elements is key to ensuring your understanding lens options are correctly applied to your new frames.

Pupillary Distance Measurement (PD): Essential for Accurate Fit

The pupillary distance measurement (PD) is an often-overlooked yet critical aspect of ordering prescription eyeglasses online. PD is the distance in millimeters between the centers of your pupils. This measurement ensures that the optical center of your lenses aligns perfectly with the center of your pupils, providing clear and comfortable vision. An incorrect PD can lead to prism effect, causing eye strain, headaches, and distorted vision, even with the correct prescription. Many online retailers simplify this process; for instance, some platforms like GlassesUSA.com offer an app with a PD scan feature, allowing you to accurately measure your PD using your smartphone camera, making the process of getting your budget-friendly spectacles both easy and precise.

Single Vision vs. Progressive Lenses Cost

For many, a simple distance prescription covers their vision needs. These single vision lenses are designed to correct vision for a single focal point, whether it's for reading, driving, or computer use. They represent the most basic and therefore the most affordable lens option. If your prescription only requires correction for one distance, you'll find these at the lower end of the price spectrum, making them ideal for truly cheap prescription glasses.

However, as we age, many people develop presbyopia, requiring different corrections for near and far vision. This is where multifocal lenses come into play. Progressive lenses (often referred to as "no-line bifocals") offer a seamless transition between distance, intermediate, and near vision without the visible line found in traditional bifocals. While incredibly convenient, the complex grinding and shaping required for progressive lenses mean they come with a significantly higher progressive lenses cost. Similarly, bifocals or lenses specifically designed to correct astigmatism also incur additional costs due to their specialized designs and manufacturing processes. These advanced lens types are more expensive but offer tailored solutions for complex vision needs.
